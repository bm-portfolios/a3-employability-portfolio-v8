About

About me

I'm partway through Level 3 of the Business Management degree, but the part of business I keep gravitating towards is the publicly-funded, not-for-profit cultural world: the people who actually run museums, galleries, theatres and festivals.

It's the management and programming side that pulls me in, not the performing. What I value professionally is audience empathy, decisions I can back with evidence, and doing things responsibly when you're accountable to the public and to funders. What you'll find on this page is my Level 3 employability work across the modules and a note on the skill behind each piece. Where I want to end up is an arts-administration role: cultural-venue or festival management, or programming.

Reflection

Reflection and next steps

Module: BSM24002 — Academic and Professional Writing and Communication

Reflection · 150 words · Atkins and Murphy's model of reflection

I'll be honest about what nagged at me before the A1 presentation. I could feel the John Lewis "Man on the Moon" ad working on me, but "it just feels moving" was all I had to defend it, and that unsettled me. I had to stand up alone and present the Age UK campaign as a properly evidenced, audience-aware argument rather than a gut reaction. Working through it, I saw why my opinion felt thin, and my willingness to learn meant picking up narrative and transportation theory, semiotics and pathos, then working independently to back each claim with Harvard sources. The harder part was judging the ethics fairly, weighing charity-linked "sadvertising" against the commercial motive, while self-managing two deadlines on 31 July. What I take into arts administration is the habit of reading the audience, asking whether a message is responsible, and proving why it will land before I put it in front of people (Atkins and Murphy, 1993).

What's next

I've put the 150-word reflection on this page too, so the portfolio and the reflection tell the same story. From here:

  • Carry the evidence-first habit and the audience-reading into anything I communicate for a venue or programme.
  • Find an arts-administration placement or volunteering (a festival, gallery or theatre) to test this in a real setting.
  • Plan earlier so two deadlines never collide like that again.
  • Update the page as new work comes through and keep it public.

BSM24003 · Contexts of the Creative Industries

Employability Skills Log

Mihaela-Julieta Diaconu — BSc (Hons) Business Management, Level 3 (Ravensbourne). Module: BSM24003 Contexts of the Creative Industries.

I've pulled this log together to show the five employability skills the module asked us to develop, and where I can actually point to each one.

Two assignments give me most of what I'm drawing on: the Task 1 recorded account I did on the theatre industry, and the Task 2 show and tell about my grandmother's carved wooden music box, a Romanian heirloom passed down the family. My plan is to work in arts and cultural-sector administration, so for each skill I've tied it back to what it takes to keep cultural venues and festivals running.

Part A — The five skills

1. Researching and placing the industry in context

For the Task 1 account I got to grips with where theatre came from: the Greek and Roman amphitheatres, the medieval mystery plays, Shakespeare and the Globe, then the West End and Broadway, plus the subsidised and repertory side the Arts Council helps keep going. Everything went into a dated timeline with a note of where it came from.

To show for it: my research notes, the reference list, and the finished theatre timeline slide.

2. Presenting out loud and through visuals

Two timed pieces: the theatre account in 10 minutes, the music box talk in about 5. For both I drafted a plan, rehearsed the timing until it fit, and worked from the slides and the object itself rather than reading straight off the page.

To show for it: a still from the recorded video, one of the theatre deck slides, the music box, and the upload link.

3. Digital and organisational competence

I filmed and exported the Task 1 video on my own, built the slide deck, and set this log up in the browser. Alongside that I looked at the everyday admin software cultural venues rely on — box-office and event-booking systems — because that's the work I'm aiming for.

To show for it: a grab of the recording software and the editing view of this page.

4. Managing my own workload to deadline

Quite a lot ran at once across the module. I got my Task 2 write-up handed in just before I gave the talk in class, then kept Task 1 and this log ticking over towards the Week 12 hand-in. What got me through was carving the work into smaller pieces and treating the rehearsal slots as fixed so they didn't slide.

To show for it: a grab of my planner and the Connect submission receipt.

5. Reflective, ethical and cultural awareness

The music box got me thinking about what a family object holds onto, and why keeping cultural things alive matters to me. The theatre research pushed me into the harder questions too: how shaky the funding can be, who actually gets access, whether the stage looks like the audience, and the carbon cost of touring big shows.

To show for it: the music box with a short caption, and the "current challenges" slide from my Task 1 deck.

Part B — Reflection

Module: BSM24003 — Contexts of the Creative Industries

Reflection · DATA model · Peters, 1991

Describe — my two big pieces were the recorded account on the theatre industry and the show and tell about my grandmother's carved wooden music box, both for BSM24003.

Analyse — between them I dug into theatre's past and turned it into a cited timeline, wrote and filmed the 10-minute account, and got the 5-minute talk ready and delivered; the slides and this log were built digitally and I kept the staggered deadlines in hand; the hardest bits were holding a talk to its time limit and keeping calm while presenting.

Theorise — that leaves me able to evidence five things the arts care about: research and contextual analysis; spoken and visual communication; digital and organisational competence; self-management; and reflective, ethical and cultural awareness.

Act — I'll carry these into arts administration, and the area I most want to work on next is feeling surer of myself when I present.

BSM24001 · Critical and Creative Thinking

Employability Skills Log

Mihaela-Julieta Diaconu — BSc (Hons) Business Management, Level 3 (Ravensbourne). Module: BSM24001 Critical and Creative Thinking — Task 3.

I'm Mihaela-Julieta Diaconu, a Business Management student heading towards arts and cultural-sector administration.

The evidence below comes from this module's MCQ quiz (Task 1) and our arts-funding debate (Task 2).

Part A — The five skills

1. Working independently and self-direction

Nobody chased me to revise for the in-class quiz. Early in the module I set my own plan across the five areas the module covers — things like framing problems, creative-thinking techniques, reflection, weighing evidence and critical questioning — and worked through them on my own. I walked into the 45-minute sitting actually ready. That is the same self-starting I would need running a venue's programme.

2. Time-management and prioritising under deadline

Quiz revision and debate prep landed on the same module timeline, so I built a weekly plan and decided what came first. Both were ready in time. There was no panicking the night before, and that mattered when two pieces of work were stacked on top of each other.

3. Persistence and following instructions

My first go at the against-the-motion case (cut the subsidy) fell apart once I checked it against the evidence. I kept reworking the argument cards into the brief's claim, evidence and reasoning shape until they held up, and I delivered the segment my group had given me. The team ended up with something they could count on.

4. Willingness to learn new tools and methods

I picked up Harvard referencing properly and learned to evaluate sources, sorting Arts Council and DCMS policy material from plain opinion. I use the same checking now when I help sort the listings for my local arts society. It meant I could cite proper academic and policy sources instead of second-guessing myself.

5. Frustration and stress management

I get nervous before speaking, and the 20-minute debate on top of the quiz made that worse. I used short rehearsals and broke the prep into small chunks so it didn't pile up on me. On the day I got my side of the arts-funding motion across clearly and on time.

Part B — Reflective Summary

Module: BSM24001 — Critical and Creative Thinking

Reflection · Boud, Keogh and Walker, 1985

1. Return to the Experience

Using Boud, Keogh and Walker's (1985) model helped me understand my skills development. I sat the quiz and then prepared and delivered my side of an arts-funding public-subsidy debate under time pressure.

2. Attend to Feelings

My confidence grew after the quiz and I was pleased when my argument for accessible culture was well received. However, I felt nerves before speaking and frustration when building counter-arguments, which was the part I struggled with most (Boud, Keogh and Walker, 1985).

3. Re-evaluate the Experience

Practising structured argument and weighing policy sources made my thinking sharper and more credible (Boud, Keogh and Walker, 1985). Deadline management and teamwork also mirrored skills needed for arts programme coordination. Moving forward, I will use revision-planning and structured argument in future modules and arts-society work.

Boud, D., Keogh, R. and Walker, D. (1985) Reflection: Turning Experience into Learning. London: Kogan Page.
